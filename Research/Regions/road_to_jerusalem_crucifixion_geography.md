# 2. Road to Jerusalem / Crucifixion Geography Research Dossier

## Core framing

For the final journey, **the strongest Gospel geography points to Jesus coming toward Jerusalem by way of the Jordan Valley/Jericho ascent**, especially in Matthew and Mark:

- Galilee
- Region of Judea “beyond the Jordan”
- Jericho
- Bethphage/Bethany/Mount of Olives
- Jerusalem

Relevant Gospel references:

- Matthew 19:1
- Matthew 20:29
- Matthew 21:1
- Mark 10:1
- Mark 10:46
- Mark 11:1

Luke preserves a long “set his face to go to Jerusalem” travel section that includes Samaritan rejection and complex movement between Samaria, Galilee, and the approach to Jerusalem:

- Luke 9:51–56
- Luke 17:11
- Luke 18:35
- Luke 19:1, 28

John shows Jesus traveling through Samaria earlier in ministry and then arriving at Bethany before Passover:

- John 4:3–5
- John 11–12

Historically, Jewish pilgrims from Galilee had several route options:

1. A shorter central route through Samaria.
2. A longer eastern route through the Jordan Valley/Perea.
3. A western/coastal route.

The central route could be roughly a three-day journey. The eastern route through Perea and Jericho could take five to seven days. Second Temple route reconstruction remains partly inferential, but the route categories are well supported by Gospel geography, Josephus, and historical-geographic scholarship.

# 3. Main Routes from Galilee to Jerusalem

## Route A — Central Samaritan Route

### 1. Biblical data

John 4 says Jesus “had to pass through Samaria” while traveling between Judea and Galilee, stopping near Sychar/Jacob’s well.

Luke 9:51–56 describes Jesus sending messengers into a Samaritan village on the way to Jerusalem, but the village refuses him because “his face was set toward Jerusalem.”

This shows the route was possible and sometimes used, but socially tense.

### 2. Historical/geographical data

The central route ran along the hill-country spine:

- Galilee / Jezreel region
- Samaria
- Shechem area
- Shiloh / Bethel region
- Judea / Jerusalem

A reconstructed pilgrimage path through Samaria could pass or skirt places such as:

- Sepphoris
- Nazareth
- Tirzah
- Shechem
- Shiloh
- Bethel

It was the shortest route, but Samaritan-Jewish hostility could make it risky, especially during pilgrimage traffic.

Josephus preserves an episode in which a Galilean was killed while Jews were traveling through Samaria to a feast, triggering wider violence.

### 3. Confidence level

**Medium-high for route existence; disputed for Jesus’ exact final use of it.**

John and Luke clearly show Samaritan travel as part of Jesus’ ministry, but Matthew/Mark’s final approach better fits the eastern/Jericho route.

### 4. Major competing views

One view emphasizes Jesus’ deliberate movement through Samaria as part of his mission across ethnic hostility.

Another sees the final Jerusalem approach as mostly Perea/Jericho, with Luke arranging travel material theologically rather than as a strict itinerary.

### 5. Game-design translation

Use this route as a **social-tension path**:

- Shorter
- Faster

- More relationally charged

The player experiences suspicion at:

- Shared roads
- Boundary zones

Possible quests:

- Finding food
- Negotiating passage
- Witnessing Jewish-Samaritan hostility
- Choosing mercy over retaliation
- Helping someone from the “wrong” side of the ethnic divide

## Route B — Eastern Route through Jordan Valley / Perea / Jericho

### 1. Biblical data

Matthew 19:1 and Mark 10:1 place Jesus leaving Galilee and going into Judea “beyond the Jordan,” which points toward Perea/east-of-Jordan movement.

Mark 10:46 and Luke 18:35–19:1 then bring Jesus to Jericho before he goes “up” to Jerusalem.

The triumphal entry begins near Bethphage and Bethany at the Mount of Olives:

- Matthew 21:1
- Mark 11:1
- Luke 19:28–29

### 2. Historical/geographical data

This route avoided Samaria by dropping eastward into the Jordan Valley, crossing through or near Perea, then re-crossing near Jericho before climbing west into Jerusalem.

It was longer but likely safer and more hospitable for Jewish pilgrims because Perea had Jewish communities.

Reconstructed route stations include:

- Beth Shean / Scythopolis
- Pella
- Sukkoth region
- Jericho
- Bethany
- Bethphage

### 3. Confidence level

**High for the final approach via Jericho; medium-high for broader Perea route.**

Jericho appears explicitly in the final approach tradition. The exact north-to-south path before Jericho is less certain.

### 4. Major competing views

Some harmonizations see Jesus crossing into Perea, ministering there, then crossing back at Jericho.

Others see the Gospel writers compressing a longer travel period into a symbolic “road to Jerusalem” rather than preserving every stop.

### 5. Game-design translation

This is the best route for the **main final-journey playable chapter**.

It gives the game:

- Heat
- Crowds
- Desert roads
- Tax collectors
- Blind beggars
- Jericho’s oasis economy
- Roman road surveillance
- The dramatic ascent to Jerusalem

### 6. Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain:

- Jordan Valley heat
- River crossings
- Reed beds
- Date palms
- Jericho oasis
- Barren Judean wilderness

Travel difficulty:

- Long route
- Hot lowlands
- Brutal climb into Judean hills

Risks:

- Dehydration
- Bandits
- Roman patrols
- Crowded Passover caravans

Food/water:

- Jordan River
- Springs
- Jericho oasis
- Date groves
- Caravan supplies

Landmarks:

- Jordan crossings
- Jericho
- Wadi Qelt / Jericho road
- Mount of Olives skyline

The Jericho-to-Jerusalem road was roughly 18 miles and involved a massive elevation change. Jerusalem sits around 2,500 feet above sea level, while Jericho is hundreds of feet below sea level. This explains the Gospel language of “going up” to Jerusalem and “going down” to Jericho.

The route passes through harsh Judean wilderness and was famous enough for Jesus’ Good Samaritan parable to assume danger from robbers.

## Antonia Fortress

### 1. Biblical data

The Antonia is not named in the Gospels, but Acts 21–23 shows Roman soldiers intervening at the Temple and taking Paul into the barracks/fortress area.

For Passion Week, Antonia matters because it represents Roman military oversight near the Temple.

### 2. Historical/geographical data

Josephus places Antonia at the northwest corner of the Temple complex and describes it as dominating the Temple area.

It functioned as a military observation and intervention point.

Its exact archaeological footprint is debated, but the general association with the Temple’s northwest area is standard.

### 3. Confidence level

**Medium-high for general location/function; disputed for exact size and reconstruction.**

### 4. Major competing views

Traditional Christian route memory associated Jesus’ trial with Antonia, making it the starting point for the Via Dolorosa.

Many modern scholars instead identify Pilate’s praetorium with Herod’s Palace on the western hill, while still seeing Antonia as a military fortress near the Temple.

### 5. Game-design translation

Use Antonia as visible Roman pressure:

- Towers
- Soldiers watching Temple crowds
- Rapid-response patrols
- Narrow stairs
- Tension whenever crowds gather

### 6. Map/worldbuilding notes

Architecture:

- Fortress/tower
- Barracks
- Watchpoints
- Access toward Temple courts

Gameplay:

- Restricted zone
- Patrol routes
- Overheard Latin/Greek commands
- Fear of riot

## Praetorium / Pilate’s Judgment Place

### Biblical data

Jesus is brought to Pilate’s praetorium.

John notes Jewish leaders avoid entering to prevent ritual defilement before Passover and mentions the Stone Pavement / Lithostrotos, called Gabbatha:

- John 18:28
- John 19:13

Mark 15:16 calls the praetorium a palace/courtyard context.

### Historical/geographical data

The traditional Via Dolorosa begins near Antonia, but many modern scholars favor Herod’s Palace on the western hill as Pilate’s praetorium when he was in Jerusalem.

Excavations around the Kishle / Tower of David area have uncovered remains associated with Herod’s palace substructures.

Jodi Magness notes the identification of Herod’s palace as praetorium is not new.

### Confidence level

**Disputed. Herod’s Palace is the stronger modern scholarly candidate; Antonia is the stronger later devotional-traditional candidate.**

### Game-design translation

Use a design solution that avoids false certainty:

- Call it “the Roman governor’s judgment place.”
- Place it in a western palace district for the historically preferred version.
- Optionally include the later Via Dolorosa as a post-biblical pilgrimage overlay in codex / encyclopedia mode.

### Map notes

- Roman standards
- Courtyard pavement
- Judgment platform / bēma
- Soldiers
- Priests outside
- Crowd pressure
- Barabbas tension

## Golgotha / Calvary

### Biblical data

Jesus is taken to “Golgotha,” meaning “Place of a Skull”:

- Matthew 27:33
- Mark 15:22
- John 19:17

It is outside or near the city, close enough for passersby and visible inscription-reading:

- John 19:20
- Hebrews 13:12

John says a garden and new tomb were nearby:

- John 19:41

### Historical/geographical data

The Church of the Holy Sepulchre is the strongest traditional and archaeological candidate.

Reasons:

- The site appears to have been outside the first-century city wall.
- It was in an old quarry / garden / burial area.
- It has early Christian veneration from the Constantinian period.

The Garden Tomb is devotionally powerful but most archaeologists date it to the Iron Age, making it difficult to match the Gospel description of a new tomb.

### Confidence level

**Medium-high for Holy Sepulchre area as best candidate; low for Garden Tomb as historical tomb; exact cross placement remains uncertain.**

### Major competing views

1. **Church of the Holy Sepulchre** — strongest historical/traditional/archaeological candidate.
2. **Garden Tomb / Gordon’s Calvary** — later Protestant devotional site, weak as exact historical tomb.
3. **Unknown nearby execution site** — cautious minimalist view.

### Game-design translation

Do not over-stage gore or make the player “do” anything to the crucifixion.

The player should arrive as witness and experience:

- Mockery
- Grief
- Fear
- Darkness
- Confession
- Silence
- Helplessness

Keep:

- Camera reverent
- Distance respectful
- Interaction limited to human care around the event

### Map notes

- Outside wall
- Quarry scarps
- Roadside execution zone
- Garden plots
- Nearby tombs
- Roman soldiers
- Grieving women
- Passersby
- City visible

# 9. Appropriate Player Actions Around Sacred Events

## Good gameplay actions

The player may:

- Carry messages between disciples, families, and safe houses.
- Help pilgrims find lodging, water, sacrificial animals, or lost relatives.
- Navigate crowd crush and blocked streets.
- Overhear debates in Temple courts.
- Help Mary / Martha / Bethany household preparations.
- Follow at a distance during the night arrest.
- Witness Peter’s fear and denial without “fixing” it.
- Help grieving followers after the crucifixion.
- Carry burial spices or news, without controlling burial itself.
- Investigate empty tomb reports as testimony spreads.
- Choose loyalty under social danger.
- Decide whether to publicly identify with Jesus’ followers.

## Actions to avoid

Do not allow the player to:

- Prevent Judas’ betrayal.
- Stop the arrest.
- Turn Gethsemane into a combat arena.
- Change the trial verdict.
- Fight Romans at the crucifixion.
- Make the crucifixion a skill challenge, QTE, boss fight, or spectacle.
- Trigger the resurrection through player success.
- Loot, desecrate, mock, or collectible-hunt at Golgotha / tomb.
- Gamify Jesus’ suffering as damage numbers, health bars, or player-driven mechanics.

# 11. Protected-Event Design

Use a **three-ring sacred event model**.

## Ring 1 — Sacred Core

Includes:

- Jesus’ arrest
- Trial
- Crucifixion
- Burial
- Resurrection proclamation

Rules:

- Non-interruptible
- Non-alterable
- Reverent
- Scripturally anchored
- No player success/failure condition changes the event

## Ring 2 — Witness Ring

The player can:

- Move
- Observe
- Hear
- Mourn
- Hide
- Follow
- Respond emotionally

The player cannot change the sacred event.

## Ring 3 — Human-Support Gameplay Ring

The player helps surrounding people:

- Disciples
- Women followers
- Pilgrims
- Servants
- Frightened families
- Confused witnesses
- Early testimony carriers

This structure lets the player feel present in the geography and pressure of Passion Week without turning the crucifixion or resurrection into ordinary gameplay.

# 16. Final Recommended Design Statement

For this section of the game, the player should experience the road from Galilee to Jerusalem as a narrowing path of pressure: familiar Galilee gives way to dangerous routes, crowded pilgrimage roads, Jericho’s last oasis, the exhausting ascent, Bethany’s fragile quiet, the Mount of Olives overlooking the city, and finally Jerusalem itself — beautiful, sacred, crowded, tense, and dangerous.

The climax should not be built around player control.

It should be built around witness.

The player helps people, carries messages, navigates fear, mourns, hides, listens, and decides whether to stand near the followers of Jesus when association becomes dangerous.

The crucifixion and resurrection are not mechanics to exploit.

They are sacred events the world bends around.

The playable question is not:

> “Can the player change what happened?”

The playable question is:

> “What does the player become after witnessing it?”
