# 3. Galilee / Capernaum Main Core Loop Geography

**Target period:** roughly the public ministry of Jesus, commonly placed in the late 20s to early 30s CE.  
**Primary map focus:** northern and northwestern Sea of Galilee, with Capernaum as the main playable hub.

## Core conclusion for the game map

Capernaum is one of the strongest possible anchors for a biblically faithful open-world life RPG. The Gospels repeatedly place Jesus’ Galilean ministry there or near there; archaeology confirms a first-century Jewish fishing village on the lake; the region naturally supports fishing, farming, toll collection, Sabbath synagogue life, household economies, road traffic, lake travel, storms, ritual purity concerns, and contact with Gentile/Decapolis territory.

The biggest caution is this: **Capernaum’s existence and general location are high confidence, but several sacred specifics are debated.** The later white limestone synagogue visible today is not the synagogue Jesus entered, though earlier remains beneath or near it may relate to a first-century public/synagogue structure. The “house of Peter” tradition is archaeologically plausible and ancient, but not provable beyond dispute.

Bethsaida’s location is also actively debated.

---

## 3.1 Historical frame: Galilee in Jesus’ ministry

### Biblical data

The Gospels present Galilee as the main early ministry zone of Jesus. Matthew says Jesus left Nazareth and settled in Capernaum “by the sea” in the region of Zebulun and Naphtali, connecting this move to Isaiah’s “Galilee of the Gentiles” / “way of the sea” language. Jesus calls fishermen along the Sea of Galilee, teaches in synagogues, heals in homes, travels by boat, withdraws to lonely places, and moves between villages and shorelines. The Capernaum complex of stories includes the synagogue, Peter’s household, fishermen, tax booth, crowds, healings, and boat travel.

Key Gospel anchors: Matthew 4:12–25; 8–9; 11:20–24; 14–15; 17:24–27; Mark 1–2; 4–6; Luke 4–7; John 2; 4; 6; 21.

### Historical/geographical data

In the early first century, Galilee was ruled not directly by a Roman prefect like Judea, but by **Herod Antipas**, a client ruler/tetrarch under Rome. Antipas ruled Galilee and Perea and founded or rebuilt urban centers such as Tiberias and Sepphoris. Josephus says Antipas built Tiberias by the Lake of Gennesaret and named it for Tiberius; Josephus also says the city’s founding was complicated by purity concerns because tombs were discovered there.

Source preserved from research response: Lexundria, Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.36 — <https://lexundria.com/j_aj/18.36/wst>

Galilee should not be designed as either “purely rural backwater” or “fully Roman cityscape.” The better reconstruction is mixed: Jewish villages, family economies, fishing and farming, local assemblies/synagogues, regional trade, tolls, debt pressure, Herodian administration, and nearby Greco-Roman cities. Modern scholarship debates whether Antipas’ urbanization created severe economic oppression or more moderate regional integration; there is no single settled model.

Source preserved from research response: Morten Hørning Jensen, “Herod Antipas in Galilee: Friend or Foe of the Historical Jesus?” — <https://www.academia.edu/6542935/Herod_Antipas_in_Galilee_Friend_or_Foe_of_the_Historical_Jesus>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** Galilee was the main Gospel ministry region; Capernaum, Nazareth, Magdala/Migdal, Tiberias, Gennesaret, and the Sea of Galilee are real geographic anchors.

**Medium confidence:** exact first-century layouts of smaller villages, local roads, toll locations, and household population sizes.

**Disputed:** Bethsaida’s precise site; the exact first-century Capernaum synagogue remains; the specific house of Peter identification.

**Unknown:** precise daily routes of Jesus between many Gospel episodes.

### Game-design translation

Build Galilee as a **dense lived-in region**, not empty Bible scenery. The player should feel that everyone is connected by family, debt, fishing rights, Sabbath rhythms, purity concerns, patronage, taxation, and reputation. Capernaum should feel small enough for everyone to know each other, but connected enough that travelers, tax collectors, traders, disciples, Pharisees, and the sick can plausibly appear there.

# 4. Capernaum / Kefar Nahum

**Source-language notes:**  
Capernaum is usually connected to Hebrew/Aramaic **Kefar Naḥum / Kfar Nahum**, “village of Nahum” or “village of comfort,” though the exact original nuance is not central to the Gospel geography. Greek forms include **Καφαρναούμ**.

## 4.1 Biblical data

Capernaum is called Jesus’ Galilean home/base in Matthew 4:13 and is strongly associated with:

- Jesus teaching in the synagogue: Mark 1:21–28; Luke 4:31–37; John 6:59.
- The house of Simon Peter and Andrew: Mark 1:29–34.
- Healings, crowds, and house scenes: Mark 2:1–12; Matthew 8–9.
- The calling of Levi/Matthew from a tax booth: Mark 2:13–17; Matthew 9:9.
- The centurion whose servant is healed: Matthew 8:5–13; Luke 7:1–10.
- Temple-tax episode involving Peter and a fish: Matthew 17:24–27.
- Woe against unrepentant Capernaum: Matthew 11:23–24; Luke 10:15.

Matthew explicitly places Jesus’ move to Capernaum “by the sea” in the territory of Zebulun and Naphtali.

Source preserved from research response: BibleRef, Matthew 4:13 — <https://www.bibleref.com/Matthew/4/Matthew-4-13.html>

## 4.2 Historical/geographical data

Capernaum is identified with the ruins at **Tell Hum / Kfar Nahum** on the northern/northwestern shore of the Sea of Galilee. Archaeological and literary evidence support a first-century fishing village. A detailed archaeological survey describes first-century Capernaum as a modest unwalled lakeside village, likely stretched in a thin strip along the shore, with an estimated population around **1,000–1,500**, though population estimates vary. It lacked major urban luxuries such as paved colonnaded streets, monumental civic buildings, and theaters.

Source preserved from research response: BYU Religious Studies Center, “Simon Peter’s Capernaum: An Archaeological Survey of the First-Century Village” — <https://rsc.byu.edu/ministry-peter-chief-apostle/simon-peter-capernaum-archaeological-survey-first-century-village>

The village architecture should be built from **dark basalt**, the local volcanic stone. Houses were probably clustered around courtyards, with grinding stones, ovens, storage jars, roof access, and work areas. Streets should not be a Roman grid; Capernaum’s first-century lanes were irregular rather than orthogonal.

Capernaum’s economy was tied to fishing, agriculture, and regional commerce. The village’s lakefront location naturally connected it to the Galilean fishing economy, and this fits the Gospel context of Peter, Andrew, James, and John.

## 4.3 Confidence level

**High confidence:** Capernaum’s location, first-century occupation, Jewish village character, fishing economy, basalt domestic architecture, and Gospel importance.

**Medium confidence:** population range, exact street plan, exact harbor/dock arrangement, exact placement of Matthew/Levi’s toll booth.

**Disputed:** whether the excavated earlier structure beneath/near the later synagogue can be confidently identified as the synagogue Jesus entered.

**Disputed but plausible:** the traditional “house of Peter.”

## 4.4 Major competing views

### Capernaum synagogue

The visible white limestone synagogue at Capernaum is late antique, not the first-century synagogue of Jesus’ day. Beneath it are basalt remains and earlier features that some scholars connect to a first-century public/synagogue building. Others warn that the remains are too fragmentary to identify securely. One archaeological survey states plainly that the New Testament indicates a synagogue existed in Capernaum, but reliable evidence for its construction, size, layout, and location does not survive with certainty.

Source preserved from research response: BYU Religious Studies Center, “Simon Peter’s Capernaum: An Archaeological Survey of the First-Century Village” — <https://rsc.byu.edu/ministry-peter-chief-apostle/simon-peter-capernaum-archaeological-survey-first-century-village>

### House of Peter

Beneath a Byzantine octagonal church, excavators found a first-century domestic structure that later received special treatment and Christian veneration. BAS summarizes the claim as possible, not proven: excavators may have uncovered the home of Peter, but certainty is not available.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “The House of Peter: The Home of Jesus in Capernaum?” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/biblical-sites-places/biblical-archaeology-sites/the-house-of-peter-the-home-of-jesus-in-capernaum/>

## 4.5 Game-design translation

Capernaum should be the **main hub village**.

Important sub-zones:

- **Player household courtyard**: cooking, storage, family tasks, reputation.
- **Fishing quarter**: nets, boats, drying racks, dock labor, hired men.
- **Market lane**: bread, oil, fish, pottery, baskets, cloth, travelers.
- **Synagogue zone**: Sabbath teaching, reading, dispute, charity, community judgment.
- **Peter/Andrew household zone**: sacred-event protected area.
- **Tax/toll point near road or dock**: Levi/Matthew story anchor.
- **Shoreline crowd area**: teaching from boat, healings, rumor gatherings.
- **Road exits**: west to Gennesaret/Magdala/Tiberias; east to Bethsaida/Jordan; uphill to Chorazin; inland route toward Cana/Nazareth.

Do **not** make Capernaum look like a Roman colony. It should feel Jewish, local, basalt-built, crowded at times, and economically pressured but not destitute.

## 4.6 Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain: low lake shore, basalt stones, small coves, reeds, fishing beaches, irregular alleys, courtyards.  
Climate: hot basin near the lake, cooler hills above.  
Economy: fish, nets, boat repair, pottery, grain, oil, tolls, small trade.  
Food: bread, fish, lentils, olives, figs, dates, wine, herbs.  
Danger zones: storms, night roads, debt collectors, illness, crowd crush, political suspicion, bandits outside town.  
Landmarks: synagogue, sacred household, toll booth, shoreline teaching zone, boat landing, road marker, basalt ovens, fish-processing area.

## 4.7 Sources to verify

Bible: Matthew 4; 8–9; 11; 17; Mark 1–2; Luke 4–7; John 6.  
Archaeology: Corbo and Loffreda excavations; BAS articles on Peter’s house and synagogue; BYU archaeological survey of first-century Capernaum.  
Modern scholarship: Jonathan Reed, Mark Chancey, Sean Freyne, Morten Hørning Jensen, K.C. Hanson, Raimo Hakola.

# 5. Sea of Galilee / Lake Kinneret / Lake Gennesaret / Sea of Tiberias

## 5.1 Biblical data

The lake is called by multiple names:

- **Sea of Galilee**: Matthew 4:18; Mark 1:16.
- **Lake of Gennesaret**: Luke 5:1.
- **Sea of Tiberias**: John 6:1; 21:1.
- Related earlier name: **Chinnereth / Kinneret** in Hebrew Bible geography.

The Gospels place many events on or beside the lake: calling fishermen, teaching from a boat, storms, crossings, demoniac/healing episodes in Gentile-adjacent territory, feeding narratives in remote areas, and resurrection appearance in John 21.

## 5.2 Historical/geographical data

The Sea of Galilee is a freshwater lake in the Jordan Rift Valley. NASA describes Lake Tiberias / Sea of Galilee as just over **21 km north-south** and about **43 m deep**, fed partly by underground springs but mainly by the Jordan River entering from the north.

Source preserved from research response: NASA Earth Observatory, Lake Tiberias / Sea of Galilee — <https://science.nasa.gov/earth/earth-observatory/lake-tiberias-sea-of-galilee-northern-israel-40147/>

The lake is about 13 km wide at its broadest and roughly 53 km around. It lies far below sea level, which helps explain its distinctive weather and sudden wind events.

Source preserved from research response: Wikipedia, Sea of Galilee — <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_of_Galilee>

A first-century wooden boat discovered in 1986 near the northwestern shore is about **8.2 m long** and **2.3 m wide**. There is no evidence connecting that specific boat to Jesus or the disciples, but it is an excellent model for the type of fishing/passenger boat used on the lake.

Source preserved from research response: Jesus Boat Museum/research page — <https://www.jesusboat.com/the-jesus-boat-research/>

Ancient fishing and shore installations were widespread. An Israel Antiquities Authority survey notes numerous Kinneret anchorages and shore installations connected to ancient fishing, many attributed to the Second Temple period.

Source preserved from research response: Israel Antiquities Authority, Kinneret shore installations — <https://hadashot.iaa.org.il/report_detail_eng.aspx?id=1766&mag_id=118>

## 5.3 Confidence level

**High confidence:** lake dimensions, freshwater character, Jordan inflow/outflow, first-century fishing, lake travel, storms, north-shore settlement density.

**Medium confidence:** exact first-century harbor layouts for each small village.

**Debated:** specific Gospel event locations on the eastern/northern shore, especially where place names vary or later traditions differ.

## 5.4 Major competing views

The lake itself is secure. The debates are mostly about **which shoreline settlement corresponds to which Gospel place**, and where specific events occurred: Bethsaida, Gergesa/Gadara/Gerasa region, feeding sites, Sermon hillside, and some boat-crossing routes.

## 5.5 Game-design translation

The Sea of Galilee should be a **major gameplay system**, not just scenery.

Core systems:

- **Boat travel:** rowing, sailing, hiring passage, cargo weight, wind.
- **Fishing:** casting net, dragnet, hook-and-line, night fishing, repair nets.
- **Weather:** calm morning, afternoon winds, sudden storm events.
- **Trade:** fresh fish locally, salted/dried fish to Magdala or inland markets.
- **Risk:** storms, broken nets, lost cargo, night navigation, boundary crossing.

Sea travel should feel useful but dangerous. A Capernaum-to-eastern-shore crossing might be a few hours in good conditions, but storms or contrary winds can turn it into a major event.

## 5.6 Map/worldbuilding notes

Fishing zones:

- **Capernaum shallows:** small boats, family teams, local market supply.
- **Gennesaret/Magdala shore:** larger trade and processing.
- **Jordan delta/Bethsaida zone:** reeds, marshy fishing, border movement.
- **Eastern shore:** Gentile-adjacent territory, cliffs/slopes, fewer Jewish village comforts.
- **Open water:** storm risk, night fishing, sacred-event protection for Gospel storm episodes.

Fish species/worldbuilding groups:

- Tilapia/musht, often associated with “St. Peter’s fish.”
- Barbels/biny.
- Sardine-like small fish.
- Catfish-like fish should be treated carefully because of Jewish food-law concerns. Modern summaries list tilapia, barbels, sardines/bleak, and other species in the lake.

Source preserved from research response: Ferrell Jenkins, fish of the Sea of Galilee — <https://ferrelljenkins.blog/2014/12/01/fish-of-the-sea-of-galilee/>

## 5.7 Sources to verify

Bible: Matthew 4; 8; 13–14; Mark 1; 4–6; Luke 5; John 6; 21.  
Ancient sources: Josephus, *War* 3; *Antiquities* 18.  
Archaeology: Ancient Galilee Boat / “Jesus Boat”; Kinneret harbor surveys; Magdala excavations.  
Modern scholarship: Mendel Nun on Galilee fishing; K.C. Hanson; Raimo Hakola.

# 6. Nearby Location Dossiers

## 6.1 Bethsaida / Bethsaida-Julias

### Biblical data

Bethsaida is associated with Philip, Andrew, and Peter in John 1:44. It appears in Gospel lake geography and in Jesus’ woe against unrepentant cities: Matthew 11:21; Luke 10:13. Mark 6:45 and Luke 9:10 also place Bethsaida in Gospel travel and feeding/crowd geography.

### Historical/geographical data

Josephus says Philip the tetrarch advanced the village Bethsaida, located by the Lake of Gennesaret, to the dignity of a city and named it **Julias**.

Source preserved from research response: Lexundria, Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.28 — <https://lexundria.com/j_aj/18.28/wst>

The main archaeological debate is between **et-Tell** and **el-Araj**, with some discussions involving nearby shoreline change and whether there may have been more than one Bethsaida-related settlement. BAS summarizes the issue clearly: two sites now vie for the identity of Bethsaida, et-Tell and el-Araj.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “Excavating el-Araj: A Candidate for Biblical Bethsaida” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/excavating-el-araj-a-candidate-for-biblical-bethsaida/>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** Bethsaida existed and was near the lake.  
**Disputed:** exact site.  
**Game solution:** include “Bethsaida district” rather than a single overconfident pin.

### Game-design translation

Use Bethsaida as an **eastern/northeastern border zone**: fishing village, Jordan delta, Philip’s territory, road toward Gaulanitis, and transition toward Gentile/Decapolis-adjacent lands.

### Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain: reeds, delta mud, shifting shoreline, fishing coves, road/ferry crossing, border traffic.  
Danger: marsh, political boundary, bandits, unfamiliar officials.  
Economy: fish, ferrying, reeds, trade tolls.

### Sources to verify

Bible: John 1:44; Mark 6:45; Luke 9:10; Matthew 11:21.  
Ancient: Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.28.  
Archaeology: et-Tell and el-Araj excavation reports.

## 6.2 Chorazin / Korazim

### Biblical data

Chorazin appears in Jesus’ woe against cities that witnessed mighty works but did not repent: Matthew 11:21; Luke 10:13. Scripture gives little geographic detail beyond its association with Capernaum and Bethsaida.

### Historical/geographical data

Chorazin is generally identified with **Korazim**, north of Capernaum on a basalt plateau. The visible synagogue ruins are later, mainly Roman/Byzantine, but recent discussions suggest earlier remains may exist beneath later structures. BAS describes Chorazin as a basalt village with a fourth-century synagogue and possible earlier Second Temple-period religious activity, but first-century Chorazin has not been excavated as fully as Capernaum or Magdala.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “Digging Through Time at Chorazin” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/biblical-sites-places/biblical-archaeology-sites/digging-through-time-at-chorazin/>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** general location north of Capernaum.  
**Medium confidence:** first-century village presence.  
**Low/disputed:** exact first-century synagogue structure.

### Game-design translation

Chorazin should be the **upland basalt village**. It gives vertical variety: climb out of humid lake basin into rugged black-stone hills, terraces, olive groves, goats, and views over the lake.

### Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain: basalt plateau, rocky fields, terraces, dry-stone walls.  
Economy: olives, grain, goats/sheep, stonework.  
Travel difficulty: short distance from Capernaum but uphill.  
Landmark: later synagogue ruins should appear only in codex/archaeological overlay, not necessarily as a fully built first-century monument.

### Sources to verify

Bible: Matthew 11:21; Luke 10:13.  
Archaeology: Korazim/Chorazin excavation reports; BAS and IAA updates.

## 6.3 Magdala / Migdal / Taricheae

### Biblical data

Magdala is associated by name with Mary Magdalene, though the Gospels do not narrate many events inside the town itself. Matthew 15:39 has a textual/place-name issue in some manuscripts involving Magadan/Magdala. Mary Magdalene appears in Luke 8:2 and in Passion/resurrection narratives.

### Historical/geographical data

Magdala/Migdal was on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee and was economically significant. Archaeology has revealed a first-century synagogue, ritual baths, marketplace/urban features, and evidence of fish-processing/trade importance. BAS notes Magdala’s first-century synagogue, ritual baths, and fishing economy; IAA material also identifies Migdal as an important Jewish city in the Second Temple period, before Tiberias, with a very early Galilean synagogue and menorah-decorated stone.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “The Fishy Secret to Ancient Magdala’s Economic Growth” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/archaeology-today/the-fishy-secret-to-ancient-magdalas-economic-growth/>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** location and first-century Jewish settlement.  
**High confidence:** first-century synagogue.  
**Medium confidence:** exact relationship between Gospel “Magdala/Magadan” textual variants and the archaeological town.  
**Unknown:** whether Mary Magdalene’s personal residence can be located.

### Game-design translation

Magdala should be the **larger fish-processing and trade hub** compared to Capernaum. It gives the player a place to sell fish in bulk, buy salt, encounter merchants, pay fees, and see a more developed lakeside economy.

### Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain: western shore, harbor installations, market streets, storage areas.  
Economy: salted/dried fish, pottery, salt, baskets, nets, trade.  
Social feel: busier, more commercial, slightly more urban than Capernaum.

### Sources to verify

Bible: Luke 8:2; Matthew 15:39 textual variants; Passion/resurrection accounts.  
Archaeology: Magdala excavations; Magdala Stone studies; IAA reports.  
Modern scholarship: Raimo Hakola on Galilean fish trade.

## 6.4 Gennesaret / Plain of Ginosar

### Biblical data

The Gospels mention the land/plain of Gennesaret as a place where Jesus and the disciples landed and where many sick people were brought to Him: Matthew 14:34–36; Mark 6:53–56. Luke uses “Lake of Gennesaret” for the Sea of Galilee in Luke 5:1.

### Historical/geographical data

Josephus praises the region around Gennesaret for fertility, mild climate, and productivity. The plain on the northwest shore of the lake is one of the most agriculturally attractive zones in the region.

Source preserved from research response: Jerusalem Perspective, “Khirbet el-‘Oreimeh / Gennesaret” — <https://www.jerusalemperspective.com/579/>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** general location on the northwestern shore.  
**Medium confidence:** precise first-century village boundaries.

### Game-design translation

Make Gennesaret the **fertile agricultural belt**: fruit, grain, springs, irrigation, gardens, sick travelers, and road traffic between Capernaum and Magdala.

### Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain: fertile plain, springs, fields, orchards, reeds near shore.  
Economy: grain, fruit, vegetables, fish exchange.  
Danger: crowd gatherings, disease, bandits on roads, land disputes.

### Sources to verify

Bible: Matthew 14:34–36; Mark 6:53–56; Luke 5:1.  
Ancient: Josephus, *War* 3.10.8.  
Maps: Galilee lake-shore maps and historical atlases.

## 6.5 Tiberias

### Biblical data

The city itself does not appear prominently in the Synoptic ministry stories, but John calls the lake the “Sea of Tiberias” in John 6:1 and John 21:1.

### Historical/geographical data

Tiberias was founded by Herod Antipas on the western shore of the lake and named for Emperor Tiberius. Josephus says it was built in the best part of Galilee by the Lake of Gennesaret, near warm baths at Emmaus/Hammat. Josephus also records Jewish purity objections because tombs were found there.

Source preserved from research response: Lexundria, Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.36 — <https://lexundria.com/j_aj/18.36/wst>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** Antipas founded Tiberias on the western shore.  
**Medium confidence:** exact early layout in the first decades after founding.  
**High confidence:** it represents Herodian/Roman administrative influence more than Capernaum does.

### Game-design translation

Tiberias should be mostly an **off-map or limited-access administrative city** in the vertical slice. Its presence should be felt through taxes, soldiers, officials, building projects, coins, and market demands. Do not make it the main village hub if the core loop is Capernaum.

### Map/worldbuilding notes

Terrain: lake city, hot springs nearby, administrative center.  
Economy: taxation, elite households, construction, fish demand, official records.  
Danger: political suspicion, soldiers, debt courts, impurity concerns.

### Sources to verify

Bible: John 6:1; 21:1.  
Ancient: Josephus, *Antiquities* 18.36–38.  
Archaeology: Tiberias excavations; Herodian coinage.

## 6.6 Cana of Galilee

### Biblical data

Cana appears in John as the place of the wedding where Jesus turns water into wine, the healing of the official’s son, and Nathanael’s hometown: John 2:1–12; 4:46–54; 21:2.

### Historical/geographical data

Cana’s location is debated. Traditional pilgrimage often points to **Kafr Kanna**, while many modern scholars argue for **Khirbet Qana** based on archaeological and geographic evidence. Other proposals exist, including Karm er-Ras. Recent IAA material notes that Karm er-Ras has archaeological evidence for an Early Roman Jewish village, while Khirbet Qana remains a major candidate in the debate.

Source preserved from research response: IAA report material — <https://publications.iaa.org.il/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1080&context=iaareports&filename=12&type=additional>

### Confidence level

**High confidence:** Cana was a Galilean village known in Johannine tradition.  
**Disputed:** exact site.  
**Game solution:** use “Cana region” or place it on an off-map route with codex explaining the debate.

### Game-design translation

Cana should be a **family/social-event location**, not a random miracle machine. The wedding at Cana should be a protected sacred-event zone if included.

### Map/worldbuilding notes

Distance: not immediate Capernaum core; better as a road-quest destination.

# 7. Political Geography

## Biblical data

The Gospels mention Herod Antipas indirectly as “Herod the tetrarch,” especially in relation to John the Baptist and later Jesus. Capernaum includes a centurion story, tax collectors, and border/toll realities. Matthew/Levi is called from a tax booth.

## Historical/geographical data

Galilee was under Herod Antipas, while territories northeast/east of the lake were associated with Philip and, farther around the lake, Decapolis/Gentile zones. This makes Capernaum strategically useful: near lake routes, roads, and political edges.

Tax systems in Roman Palestine included direct taxes and indirect taxes such as tolls/customs/sales taxes, often handled at provincial, municipal, or local levels.

Source preserved from research response: Cambridge University Press, *Class and Power in Roman Palestine*, taxation chapter — <https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/class-and-power-in-roman-palestine/taxation/895C51C0C1CE035D8591EEA76844BCF4>

The fishing economy was likely not a modern free market. K.C. Hanson models Galilean fishing as embedded in political and household systems involving rulers, tax administrators, brokers, collectors, fishing families, hired workers, processors, and shippers.

Source preserved from research response: K.C. Hanson, Galilean fishing economy article — <https://radicalbible.net/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/Galilean-fishing-economy-article.pdf>

## Confidence level

**High confidence:** Antipas ruled Galilee; tolls/taxes existed; tax collectors were socially controversial.  
**Medium confidence:** exact toll rates and local administrative arrangements in Capernaum.  
**Disputed:** degree of economic oppression versus regional opportunity.

## Game-design translation

Make politics felt through systems:

- Fishing permits or tolls.
- Tax checkpoint near road/dock.
- Herodian official visits from Tiberias.
- Rumors about John the Baptist.
- Soldiers or armed retainers, but not everywhere.
- Border tension near Bethsaida/Philip territory.
- Roman influence mostly indirect in Capernaum, stronger near Tiberias/Sepphoris.

Do not fill Capernaum with legionaries like a Roman fort town. A centurion can exist in the story world, but ordinary village governance should feel Jewish/local under Herodian-Roman pressure.

# 8. Religious Geography

## Biblical data

Jesus teaches in synagogues throughout Galilee. Sabbath synagogue scenes are central in Capernaum and Nazareth. The Gospels include Pharisees, scribes, questions about fasting, purity, Sabbath, table fellowship, and authority. Pilgrimage to Jerusalem appears in the wider Gospel world.

## Historical/geographical data

First-century synagogues were community assembly spaces, not necessarily the monumental basilica-style buildings of later centuries. Confirmed pre-135 CE synagogue sites in Judea/Galilee include Capernaum, Magdala, Gamla, Herodium, Masada, and others; BAS notes a growing list of early synagogue evidence.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “Jesus and Synagogues” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/biblical-topics/new-testament/jesus-and-synagogues/>

Jewish ritual purity concerns are visible archaeologically through stone vessels and ritual baths/mikvaot in many first-century Jewish contexts. BAS notes chalk/limestone vessels became popular because Torah interpreters considered stone resistant to ritual impurity.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “Archaeology and Jewish Purity Practices” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/ancient-cultures/daily-life-and-practice/archaeology-and-jewish-purity-practices/>

## Confidence level

**High confidence:** synagogue life, Sabbath rhythms, Torah reading, purity concerns, pilgrimage consciousness.  
**Medium confidence:** exact synagogue liturgy in each village.  
**Low/unknown:** precise Pharisee/scribe presence in every Galilean village at every moment.

## Game-design translation

Religious life should structure the calendar:

- Sabbath changes available work.
- Market slows or closes.
- Cooking/preparation happens before Sabbath.
- Synagogue gathering becomes social/legal/religious hub.
- Purity issues affect vessels, meals, sick/outcast contact, burial, and pilgrimage preparation.
- Festivals create travel routes to Jerusalem.

Avoid making Pharisees cartoon villains. Some are opponents, some are curious, some are sincere Torah teachers. The social tension is about authority, purity, interpretation, honor, and identity under occupation.

# 9. Social Geography

## Main social groups

### Fishermen

Fishing families should work as households and partnerships, not lone adventurers. Zebedee’s hired servants in Mark 1:20 suggest some families had more resources than bare subsistence. Nets, boats, hired men, processors, tolls, and buyers form a network.

### Farmers

Fields, terraces, vineyards, olive groves, fig trees, grain patches, and threshing floors should dominate inland Galilee. Even lakeside villages likely combined fishing with farming.

### Craftsmen

Carpenters/builders, stonecutters, potters, net-makers, basket-makers, leatherworkers, and boat repairers are plausible. “Tekton” work should not be limited to fine carpentry; it can involve construction and practical building labor.

### Tax collectors

Tax collectors are socially powerful and morally suspect in Gospel settings. The player should see why people resent them: tolls on fish, roads, market goods, and debt obligations.

### Women’s daily work

Women’s work should be central: grinding grain, baking bread, drawing water, textile work, food preservation, child care, hospitality, market exchange, ritual preparation, mourning, and festival preparation.

### Sick/outcast people

The Gospels repeatedly show the sick, demon-oppressed, lepers, disabled, widows, and socially excluded coming near Jesus. In gameplay, they should not be reduced to “quest items.” They are part of the moral and social world.

### Patronage, debt, and poverty

Players should feel pressure from debt, harvest failure, illness, taxes, and kinship obligations. But Galilee should not be designed as uniformly starving. The region had productive land, fishing, and trade, while still being vulnerable to extraction and instability.

# 10. Travel Routes

## Around the lake

Main local routes:

- Capernaum west to Gennesaret/Magdala/Tiberias.
- Capernaum east/northeast to Bethsaida/Jordan crossing.
- Capernaum uphill to Chorazin.
- Lake crossings to eastern shore/Decapolis-adjacent areas.
- Inland routes toward Cana, Nazareth, Sepphoris, Nain, and Jezreel.

Capernaum likely benefited from proximity to major routes. The traditional “Via Maris” language is complicated; some scholars prefer “International North-South Route” or more specific route names, but first-century Galilee clearly sat near important corridors connecting the lake, Damascus, the coast, and inland regions.

Source preserved from research response: Nazareth Village, first-century travel — <https://nazarethvillage.com/about/research-and-archaeology/first-century-travel/>

## Galilee to Jerusalem

There were multiple pilgrimage/travel options:

1. **Through Samaria** — shortest, perhaps around three days under favorable conditions, but socially/politically tense.
2. **Jordan Valley / Perea route** — longer but often preferred by Jews avoiding Samaria.
3. **Western/Jezreel-coastal variants** — possible depending on origin and destination.

BAS summarizes three Galilee-to-Jerusalem pilgrimage paths and notes the central Samaritan route was shortest but not always preferred.

Source preserved from research response: Biblical Archaeology Society, “3 Pilgrimage Paths from Galilee to Jerusalem” — <https://www.biblicalarchaeology.org/daily/3-pilgrimage-paths-from-galilee-to-jerusalem/>

## Game-design translation

For the vertical slice, do not fully build Jerusalem. Use road exits and travel screens:

- “Road to Nazareth/Cana.”
- “Road to Tiberias.”
- “Road to Bethsaida/Philip’s territory.”
- “Pilgrim road south.”
- “Jordan crossing.”

Travel should include risk: heat, bandits, tolls, Samaritan tension, water scarcity, illness, and lodging uncertainty.

# 11. Environment and Seasonal Worldbuilding

## Terrain

Galilee gives excellent map variety in a small area:

- Lake basin below sea level.
- Reedy shores and fishing beaches.
- Fertile Gennesaret plain.
- Basalt uplands near Chorazin.
- Terraced hills toward Nazareth/Cana.
- Jordan delta/marsh.
- Eastern slopes and Gentile-adjacent regions.
- Caves, tombs, wilderness edges, lonely prayer places.

## Weather and seasons

The region has a Mediterranean seasonal rhythm: rainy season roughly autumn through spring; dry season in summer. Dry-season rainfall is rare, while grain and fruit cycles shape food, labor, and festivals.

Source preserved from research response: Archdiocese of Washington blog, climate in Jesus’ time — <https://blog.adw.org/2017/03/climate-like-time-jesus/>

The Sea of Galilee’s basin geography makes sudden storms plausible. Cool air from surrounding heights can rush down into the warm lake basin, creating violent wind events.

Source preserved from research response: That the World May Know, Sea of Galilee geography — <https://www.thattheworldmayknow.com/sea-of-galilee-geography>

## Game-design translation

Seasonal systems:

- **Winter/rainy season:** muddy roads, greener hills, dangerous lake winds, fuller cisterns.
- **Spring:** grain growth, Passover pilgrimage preparations, crowds.
- **Early summer:** harvest, threshing, heat, dry grass.
- **Late summer/autumn:** grapes, figs, olives, water pressure, Sukkot pilgrimage.

# 12. Representing Jesus’ Ministry Without Making Jesus a Normal Sandbox NPC

Jesus should not be treated like a recruitable companion, shopkeeper, romance option, combat ally, or generic quest dispenser.

Better model: **Sacred Presence System**

- Jesus appears in protected narrative sequences.
- The player can witness, serve nearby, respond, carry messages, prepare spaces, manage crowds, provide food, row boats, or care for family.
- The player cannot command Jesus, summon Him, interrupt sacred teaching, loot miracle sites, or turn miracles into repeatable mechanics.
- Rumors spread ahead of Him and after Him.
- NPCs react differently: faith, curiosity, fear, resentment, political concern, desperation.
- Sacred events reshape the social world afterward.

This keeps Jesus central without reducing Him to an ordinary sandbox actor.

# 17. Suggested Opening Vertical-Slice Quest Chain

## Act 1 — “A Day in Kefar Nahum”

Player wakes in a family courtyard. Tasks: grind grain, fetch water, repair net, deliver fish to market. Introduces household economy, food, kinship, and village reputation.

## Act 2 — “The Catch and the Toll”

Player joins a fishing crew. Catch must be sorted: household portion, market portion, tax/toll portion. Player encounters Levi/Matthew’s booth and sees why tax collectors are hated.

## Act 3 — “Sabbath in the Synagogue”

Work rhythm changes. Player attends synagogue. Sacred event is protected. Afterward the village reacts: awe, fear, debate, rumors.

## Act 4 — “Crowds at the Door”

Capernaum becomes crowded with the sick and desperate. Player helps manage hospitality, water, crowd movement, family strain, and moral choices.

## Act 5 — “Across the Lake”

Boat travel opens. Weather system introduced. A sacred crossing event is protected; normal crossings remain sandbox systems.

## Act 6 — “Roads Beyond the Village”

Route nodes unlock: Chorazin heights, Gennesaret plain, Bethsaida border, Magdala trade route. The player learns Galilee is not a static village but a network.

# 21. Accuracy and Uncertainty Notes

This archive preserves the research and design work generated in the conversation. Several claims are intentionally marked as debated or uncertain:

- The visible Capernaum synagogue is later than the ministry of Jesus; any first-century synagogue remains beneath/near it are debated.
- The house of Peter tradition is ancient and archaeologically plausible but not provable beyond dispute.
- Bethsaida’s location remains debated, especially between et-Tell and el-Araj.
- Cana’s location remains debated, with Kafr Kanna, Khirbet Qana, and other candidates.
- The exact Mount of Beatitudes/Sermon location is traditional rather than archaeologically certain.
- Travel routes to Jerusalem varied; through Samaria, around Samaria via the Jordan/Perea, and other route variants should all be represented as plausible options.
- The game should avoid making sacred events repeatable mechanics or falsely precise map pins where the evidence does not justify certainty.

# 22. Project Design Takeaway

The Capernaum/Galilee vertical slice should function as the main testbed for the entire biblical life RPG design philosophy:

- The player is a witness, not the savior.
- Jesus’ ministry is sacred and protected, not sandboxed.
- Ordinary life is meaningful: fishing, debt, food, family, Sabbath, reputation, illness, hospitality, fear, and faith.
- Geography matters: lake, road, village, hill, synagogue, toll booth, field, and wilderness all shape player experience.
- Historical uncertainty belongs in the codex, not hidden under false confidence.
