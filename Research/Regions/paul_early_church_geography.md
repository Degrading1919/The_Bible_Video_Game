## 3. Assistant Research Response — Paul / Early Church Geography

# Paul / Early Church Geography — Acts & Epistles World-Research Dossier

## Working frame

Paul’s world should not feel like isolated “Bible locations.” It should feel like a **Roman imperial travel network** overlaid on older Jewish, Greek, Anatolian, Syrian, Phoenician, and local identities. Acts moves through synagogues, markets, roads, ports, courts, households, workshops, prisons, temples, theaters, and governor’s halls. That is the gameplay geography.

Acts gives the narrative spine. Paul’s letters give internal checkpoints. Roman roads, sea routes, provincial boundaries, inscriptions, archaeology, and later traditions help fill the world, but they do not all have the same certainty.

---

# 1. Macro Route Structure

## Paul’s main route arcs

### Arc 1 — Jerusalem / Damascus / Syria / Cilicia

**Core biblical movement:**  
Jerusalem → Damascus → Arabia/return to Damascus → Jerusalem → Caesarea → Tarsus → Syrian Antioch.

**Biblical data:** Acts 7–9, Acts 11, Acts 22, Acts 26, Galatians 1–2.

**Design meaning:** This is Paul’s transformation arc, but the player should experience it indirectly: rumors, frightened believers, synagogue tension, Damascus road reports, letters of authorization, and later testimony.

---

### Arc 2 — First Missionary Journey

**Route:** Syrian Antioch → Seleucia Pieria → Cyprus: Salamis and Paphos → Perga in Pamphylia → Pisidian Antioch → Iconium → Lystra → Derbe → return through the same towns → Attalia → Syrian Antioch.

**Biblical data:** Acts 13–14.

**Design meaning:** Best for “frontier mission” gameplay: hard overland routes, synagogue-first preaching, Gentile reception, local mobs, confused pagan worship, stoning at Lystra, new house-church cells.

---

### Arc 3 — Second Missionary Journey

**Route:** Syrian Antioch → Syria/Cilicia → Derbe/Lystra/Iconium region → Phrygia/Galatia → Troas → Samothrace → Neapolis → Philippi → Thessalonica → Berea → Athens → Corinth → Cenchreae → Ephesus → Caesarea/Jerusalem → Antioch.

**Biblical data:** Acts 15:36–18:22.

**Design meaning:** This is the best “open-world travel campaign.” It crosses Anatolian roads, Aegean sea lanes, Macedonia’s Via Egnatia corridor, Greek intellectual culture, and Roman legal systems.

---

### Arc 4 — Third Missionary Journey

**Route:** Antioch → Galatia/Phrygia → Ephesus → Macedonia → Greece/Achaia, likely Corinth → return through Macedonia → Philippi → Troas → Assos → Mitylene → Chios → Samos → Miletus → Cos → Rhodes → Patara → Tyre → Ptolemais → Caesarea → Jerusalem.

**Biblical data:** Acts 18:23–21:17.

**Design meaning:** This should feel like a mature church-network arc: collecting aid, sending letters, appointing elders, resolving doctrinal threats, coordinating couriers, and avoiding ambush.

---

### Arc 5 — Arrest, Caesarea, Shipwreck, Rome

**Route:** Jerusalem arrest → Caesarea imprisonment → Sidon → Myra → Cnidus → Crete/Fair Havens → storm → Malta → Syracuse → Rhegium → Puteoli → Forum of Appius / Three Taverns → Rome.

**Biblical data:** Acts 21–28.

**Design meaning:** This is the strongest travel-survival/legal-drama arc: temple riot, Roman custody, hearings before Felix/Festus/Agrippa, appeal to Caesar, winter sailing danger, shipwreck, healing on Malta, and arrival in Rome.

# 3. Timeline and Letter Geography

Dates are approximate. The Gallio inscription at Delphi is the strongest external chronological anchor for Paul because it connects Gallio, proconsul of Achaia in Acts 18, to the early 50s CE; many reconstructions place Paul’s Corinth ministry around 50–52 CE. Source used in original response: Bible Odyssey, “Erastus, Gallio, and Paul” — <https://www.bibleodyssey.org/articles/erastus-gallio-and-paul/>

| Period | Likely date range | Geography | Letters / events |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Conversion and early years | c. 33–36 CE, disputed | Damascus, Arabia, Jerusalem, Tarsus | Galatians 1; Acts 9 |
| Antioch formation | c. 40s | Syrian Antioch, Jerusalem | Acts 11; famine relief; Gentile mission base |
| First journey | c. 46–48/49 | Cyprus and southern Galatia | Acts 13–14 |
| Jerusalem Council | c. 48/49 | Jerusalem | Acts 15; Galatians 2 relationship debated |
| Second journey | c. 49/50–52 | Anatolia, Macedonia, Achaia | 1–2 Thessalonians likely from Corinth |
| Third journey | c. 53–57 | Ephesus, Macedonia, Greece | 1 Corinthians from Ephesus; 2 Corinthians from Macedonia; Romans likely from Corinth/Cenchreae |
| Arrest and Caesarea | c. 57–59 | Jerusalem, Caesarea | Acts 21–26 |
| Voyage and Rome | c. 59–62 | Mediterranean, Malta, Rome | Philippians, Philemon, Colossians, Ephesians often placed in Roman imprisonment, though Ephesus/Caesarea theories exist |
| Later ministry? | after Acts, disputed | Crete, Ephesus, Nicopolis, Spain? | Pastorals depend on accepting post-Acts release; Spain is hoped for in Romans 15 but not narrated in Acts |

**Major scholarly uncertainty:**  
Galatians may be written to churches in the Roman province of Galatia founded in Acts 13–14, the “South Galatian” view, or to ethnic Galatia farther north, the “North Galatian” view. For game design, use the South Galatian churches as the playable Galatian network, but mark the letter destination debate in codex entries.

# 4. Travel Systems

## Roads

Roman roads were not modern highways, but they gave the empire a reliable skeleton: paved or prepared surfaces, milestones, bridges, army use, taxation, courier movement, inns, and road stations. ORBIS is useful for modeling relative time and cost across Roman roads, rivers, and sea routes, but it reconstructs the mature imperial network around 200 CE, so use it as an approximation rather than a perfect Paul-era map. Source used in original response: ORBIS Stanford Roman travel model — <https://orbis.stanford.edu/>

**Gameplay use:** road fatigue, pack animals, tolls, inns, official checkpoints, local guides, Roman patrols, bandits, weather delays, and seasonal route closures.

## Sea routes

Paul’s sea travel usually follows commercial movement, not private missionary ships. He boards available merchant vessels, including Alexandrian grain ships in Acts 27–28. The voyage to Rome is especially detailed: Myra, Cnidus, Crete, Fair Havens, storm, Malta, Syracuse, Rhegium, Puteoli. Bible Mapper’s reconstruction follows the Acts sequence and emphasizes the failed attempt to winter safely before the shipwreck. Source used in original response: Bible Mapper, “Paul’s Voyage to Rome” — <https://biblemapper.com/blog/index.php/2021/05/24/pauls-voyage-to-rome/>

**Gameplay use:** booking passage, ship cargo, sailors’ superstition, seasonal sailing windows, storms, prisoners under guard, shipboard hierarchy, grain shipments, emergency cargo loss.

## Dangers

Paul himself lists dangers from rivers, robbers, Jews, Gentiles, cities, wilderness, sea, false brothers, labor, hunger, cold, and exposure in 2 Corinthians 11:23–28. Do not make the world “safe because it is Roman.” Roman order reduces some danger, but local mobs, legal vulnerability, banditry, storms, and social hostility remain central.

## Legal status

Paul’s Roman citizenship matters in Philippi, Jerusalem, Caesarea, and Rome. It should function as a high-impact legal mechanic: it does not prevent suffering, but it changes what authorities may legally do to him.

**Gameplay use:** citizenship documents, appeal rights, magistrate fear, unlawful beating consequences, prison conditions, escort missions.

# 5. City-by-City Location Dossiers

## 5.1 Jerusalem — יְרוּשָׁלַיִם / Ἱεροσόλυμα

**1. Biblical data**  
Jerusalem is where Paul studied under Gamaliel, persecuted believers, later met apostles, participated in famine relief, attended the Jerusalem Council, returned with collection funds, and was arrested after temple accusations. Key texts: Acts 7–9, 11:27–30, 15, 21–23; Galatians 1–2; Romans 15:25–31.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
First-century Jerusalem was the Temple-centered heart of Jewish identity under Roman oversight. For Paul’s geography, the key spaces are the Temple courts, Antonia Fortress, council chambers, pilgrim streets, pools, markets, and routes down to Caesarea.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Jerusalem as a central location. Medium for exact movement inside the city in many scenes. Low for precise house locations of early believers unless named traditions are later.

**4. Major competing views**  
The biggest disputes are chronological: how Galatians 2 relates to Acts 11 or Acts 15, and how many Jerusalem visits Paul made before the Council.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Jerusalem should be politically dense, not geographically huge. Build it as a high-stakes hub with layered access: Temple courts, Jewish leadership zones, Roman military oversight, believer safe houses, public stairs, and riot-prone chokepoints.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Dry hill-country climate, pilgrimage crowd surges, narrow streets, water systems, food vendors, sacrificial economy, Roman surveillance, theological debate, rumor networks.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 7–9, 15, 21–23; Galatians 1–2; Josephus, *Jewish War* and *Antiquities*; Jerusalem archaeology; Leen Ritmeyer, Shimon Gibson, E. P. Sanders, Craig Keener.

---

## 5.2 Damascus — דַּמֶּשֶׂק / Δαμασκός

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul receives authority to arrest believers in Damascus, encounters the risen Jesus on the road, is blinded, healed through Ananias, baptized, preaches in synagogues, and escapes over the wall in a basket. Key texts: Acts 9, 22, 26; 2 Corinthians 11:32–33; Galatians 1:17.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Damascus was an ancient oasis city on major caravan routes. Acts names “Straight Street,” which fits the idea of a formal urban street axis, though exact house identifications are later tradition.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Damascus as location. Medium for Straight Street’s general urban corridor. Low for exact house of Ananias or wall escape spot.

**4. Major competing views**  
Paul’s “Arabia” in Galatians 1:17 is debated. It may mean Nabataean territory near Damascus/Transjordan/Petra, a desert retreat, or a broader Arabian region. Do not map it with certainty.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Make Damascus a conversion-aftermath hub: synagogue shock, believers afraid of Saul, city gates watched, underground escape route, desert-edge travel.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Oasis gardens, caravanserai, city walls, Jewish quarter/synagogue presence, desert road approaches, Nabataean/Roman political ambiguity.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 9, 22, 26; Galatians 1; 2 Corinthians 11; Josephus for regional politics; Strabo for Nabataean context; modern Pauline chronology studies.

---

## 5.3 Syrian Antioch — Ἀντιόχεια ἡ ἐπὶ Ὀρόντου

**1. Biblical data**  
Believers fleeing persecution preach at Antioch; disciples are first called Christians there; Barnabas brings Saul there; prophets and teachers worship and fast; Paul and Barnabas are sent from Antioch and return there after journeys. Key texts: Acts 11:19–30; 13:1–3; 14:26–28; 15; 18:22–23; Galatians 2.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Antioch on the Orontes was one of the great cities of the Roman East, capital of Roman Syria, connected to land and sea routes, culturally mixed, and supplied by fertile countryside and nearby maritime access. The Worcester Art Museum summary notes Antioch’s trade-route importance, rich food supply, public infrastructure, Roman transformation, religious diversity, and suitability as a mission base. Source used in original response: Worcester Art Museum Antioch archive — <https://archive.worcesterart.org/exhibitions/coa.html>

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Antioch’s role as Gentile mission base. High for broad geography. Medium for exact meeting-house locations.

**4. Major competing views**  
The main debate is not location but ecclesial chronology: how the Antioch incident in Galatians 2 relates to Acts 15 and the Jerusalem Council.

**5. Game-design translation**  
This should be the main early-church operations hub: mission board, prayer gatherings, scribe house, supply market, Jewish-Gentile table fellowship conflict, language diversity.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Orontes River, fertile Amuq plain, road to Seleucia Pieria port, colonnaded streets, baths, fountains, theaters, temples, synagogues, mixed Greek/Syriac/Jewish/Roman population.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 11–15, 18; Galatians 2; Libanius for later Antioch civic life; Worcester Art Museum Antioch archive; Glanville Downey, *A History of Antioch in Syria*.

---

## 5.4 Tarsus and Cilicia — Ταρσός / Κιλικία

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul identifies himself as a Jew from Tarsus in Cilicia, “a citizen of no obscure city.” He is sent to Tarsus after danger in Jerusalem and later works through Syria and Cilicia. Key texts: Acts 9:30; 11:25; 21:39; 22:3; 15:41.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Tarsus sat in the Cilician plain near the Cydnus River and important routes through the Taurus Mountains. It was known as a significant urban and intellectual center.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Paul’s connection to Tarsus. Medium for detailed reconstruction of Paul’s family status and exact upbringing.

**4. Major competing views**  
How Paul’s family acquired Roman citizenship is unknown. Do not invent a definitive backstory.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Use Tarsus as a codex-rich homeland hub or prologue location: tentmaking/leatherworking economy, Greek education, Jewish diaspora life, Roman civic pride.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Fertile plain, river, mountain pass routes, trade warehouses, artisan workshops, Cilician sailors/merchants, rugged road north through Cilician Gates.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 9, 11, 15, 21–22; Strabo; Cicero; modern studies of Cilicia and Pauline background.

---

## 5.5 Seleucia Pieria — Σελεύκεια Πιερία

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul and Barnabas sail from Seleucia to Cyprus after being sent out from Antioch. Key text: Acts 13:4.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Seleucia Pieria was the port serving Syrian Antioch near the mouth of the Orontes. It functioned as Antioch’s maritime outlet.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for general location. Medium for exact harbor conditions in Paul’s day.

**4. Major competing views**  
No major biblical location dispute; the main issue is how much of the Roman harbor should be visible depending on your chosen date.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Make Seleucia a departure gate: ship contracts, dockside cargo, sailors, customs officials, fishermen, soldiers, and believers praying before departure.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Coastal cliffs/hills, harbor works, Orontes access, warehouses, ship repair, sea birds, sailors’ taverns, Roman administrative presence.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 13:4; Barrington Atlas; Pleiades; Antioch/Seleucia archaeological studies.

---

## 5.6 Cyprus — Κύπρος: Salamis and Paphos

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul, Barnabas, and John Mark land at Salamis, proclaim in Jewish synagogues, cross the island to Paphos, confront Bar-Jesus/Elymas, and encounter the proconsul Sergius Paulus. Key text: Acts 13:4–12.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Cyprus was a Roman senatorial province governed by a proconsul, matching Luke’s title in Acts. Paphos was the administrative capital in the Roman period and a major religious center connected with Aphrodite worship.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Salamis-to-Paphos route in Acts. High for proconsular status generally. Medium to disputed for identifying inscriptions with the exact Sergius Paulus of Acts.

**4. Major competing views**  
The exact road line across Cyprus and the precise official residence scene are reconstructed. Do not claim a specific palace room.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Cyprus works as a self-contained island chapter: port arrival, synagogue preaching, overland road, occult/political confrontation, Roman governor’s court.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Eastern port at Salamis, westward road, inland villages, copper trade background, vineyards/olive groves, coastal sanctuaries, Greek-speaking cities, Jewish diaspora presence.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 13; inscriptions on Roman Cyprus; Paphos archaeology; Roman provincial lists; Bruce, Hemer, Keener.

---

## 5.7 Perga and Pamphylia — Πέργη / Παμφυλία

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul’s party reaches Perga after Cyprus; John Mark leaves and returns to Jerusalem. Later Paul and Barnabas preach in Perga before sailing from Attalia. Key texts: Acts 13:13; 14:24–26; 15:38.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Perga was an inland city of Pamphylia connected to the southern Anatolian coast. Moving from Pamphylia up to Pisidian Antioch required a difficult climb into the Taurus/Pisidian highlands.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Perga’s location. Medium for exact motives behind John Mark’s departure.

**4. Major competing views**  
The reason Mark left is unknown: danger, illness, disagreement, homesickness, or route difficulty are all conjectures.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Use Perga as a transition point from coastal mission to dangerous inland road. This is where the party’s unity breaks.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Hot coastal plain, marshy/riverine approaches, road north into mountains, brigand risk, local temples, traders moving between coast and plateau.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 13–15; Barrington Atlas; southern Anatolia route studies; Mark Wilson on Pauline routes in Asia Minor.

## 5.8 Pisidian Antioch — Ἀντιόχεια τῆς Πισιδίας

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul preaches in the synagogue; many Jews and God-fearing Gentiles respond; opposition arises; Paul and Barnabas turn explicitly toward Gentiles; persecution drives them out. Key text: Acts 13:14–52.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Pisidian Antioch was a Roman colony in the highlands of central Anatolia and a key point on Roman road systems in the region.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for broad location and biblical role. Medium for exact synagogue location.

**4. Major competing views**  
Part of the Galatians debate: were these South Galatian churches the recipients of Galatians? Strongly possible, but not unanimous.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Make this the first major Anatolian hub: synagogue sermon, Gentile crowd growth, elite women and city leaders stirred up, exile from town.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
High plateau, cooler climate than Pamphylia, Roman colony architecture, veterans, Latin civic identity mixed with Greek/local culture, grain fields, mountain roads.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 13; Galatians; Ramsay, Bruce, Longenecker, Mark Wilson, Barrington Atlas.

## 5.9 Iconium — Ἰκόνιον

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul and Barnabas preach in the synagogue; Jews and Greeks believe; opposition divides the city; threat of mistreatment and stoning causes them to flee. Key text: Acts 14:1–7.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Iconium was an important central Anatolian city, located on major inland routes.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for location. Medium for exact political status in Paul’s time.

**4. Major competing views**  
Whether Iconium should be grouped culturally with Phrygia, Lycaonia, or Galatia shifts depending on administrative period and author.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Use Iconium as a divided-city mission: public debate, factional tension, surveillance, escape before mob violence.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Inland plain, road crossroads, market squares, synagogue, local elites, Roman officials, surrounding agricultural villages.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 14; Barrington Atlas; Asia Minor provincial studies.

## 5.10 Lystra — Λύστρα

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul heals a lame man; locals mistake Barnabas for Zeus and Paul for Hermes; Paul is stoned and dragged outside the city; Timothy later comes from Lystra. Key texts: Acts 14:8–20; 16:1–3; 2 Timothy 3:11.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Lystra was a Roman colony in Lycaonia. Acts preserves the local pagan reaction vividly, suggesting a setting less synagogue-centered than Pisidian Antioch or Iconium.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for general location. Medium for exact site details. High for Lystra’s importance to Timothy’s background.

**4. Major competing views**  
The exact social-linguistic setting is debated; Acts mentions the Lycaonian language, which should not be reduced to generic Greek speech.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Make Lystra culturally distinct. This is where a miracle produces misunderstanding instead of immediate clarity.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Smaller colony feel, local language, Zeus/Hermes cult imagery, rural roads, stones outside city, Timothy’s mixed Jewish-Greek household.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 14, 16; 2 Timothy 3; inscriptions from Lycaonia; Barrington Atlas; Ramsay and later corrections.

---

## 5.11 Derbe — Δέρβη

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul and Barnabas preach in Derbe and make many disciples before returning through Lystra, Iconium, and Antioch to strengthen churches. Key text: Acts 14:20–21.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Derbe was an eastern Lycaonian/Galatian frontier city. Its exact archaeological identification has been debated.

**3. Confidence level**  
Medium. The broad region is clear; the exact site is debated.

**4. Major competing views**  
Several site identifications have been proposed in southern central Turkey. Use a “Derbe region” marker unless your map commits to one scholarly site.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Use Derbe as the farthest eastern edge of the first journey: safer reception, disciple-making, then the hard decision to revisit hostile towns.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Frontier roads, open plateau, villages, fewer monumental assets than Ephesus or Corinth, caravan traffic, rural believers.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 14; Barrington Atlas; Mark Wilson; site-identification literature on Derbe.

## 5.12 Troas — Τρῳάς

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul receives the Macedonian vision at Troas; later Eutychus falls from a window during a night gathering. Key texts: Acts 16:8–11; 20:5–12; 2 Corinthians 2:12; 2 Timothy 4:13.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Alexandria Troas was a major port on the northwest Anatolian coast, facing the Aegean routes toward Macedonia.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for general location and port role. Medium for exact house/church setting.

**4. Major competing views**  
No major dispute on the broad location; the vision itself is theological/biblical, not reducible to route strategy.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Troas should be a liminal “decision port”: blocked routes behind, sea ahead, vision, waiting for ships, night worship scene.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Harbor, warehouses, sailors, wind/weather schedule, old Trojan-region cultural memory, road west from Asia Minor interior.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 16, 20; 2 Corinthians 2; 2 Timothy 4; Barrington Atlas; Pleiades.

## 5.13 Philippi — Φίλιπποι

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul first preaches at a riverside prayer place; Lydia believes; a slave girl is delivered; Paul and Silas are beaten and imprisoned; earthquake opens prison; jailer’s household believes; magistrates fear because Paul and Silas are Roman citizens. Key texts: Acts 16; Philippians.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Philippi lay in northeastern Greece on the Via Egnatia at the foot of an acropolis. UNESCO describes it as a walled city refounded by Philip II and later reshaped as a Roman colony, a “small Rome,” with forum, theater, and later Christian basilicas tied to Paul’s visit. Source used in original response: UNESCO, Archaeological Site of Philippi — <https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1517/>

**3. Confidence level**  
High for city and route. High for Roman colony setting. Medium/low for exact “Paul’s prison” tourist identifications.

**4. Major competing views**  
The riverside prayer place location is plausible but not archaeologically fixed with certainty. Later prison identifications are traditional.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Philippi is a top-tier playable hub: Roman colony pride, lack or smallness of synagogue, women’s prayer gathering, exorcism conflict, illegal beating, prison miracle, citizenship reversal.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Via Egnatia cutting through/near city, forum, theater, acropolis, nearby river/stream, Roman veteran culture, purple-goods trade through Lydia, prison interior, magistrate hall.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 16; Philippians; UNESCO Philippi; inscriptions; Craig Keener; Peter Oakes.

## 5.14 Thessalonica — Θεσσαλονίκη

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul preaches in the synagogue for three Sabbaths; some Jews, many devout Greeks, and leading women believe; mob attacks Jason’s house; believers send Paul and Silas away by night. Key texts: Acts 17:1–9; 1–2 Thessalonians.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Thessalonica was a major Macedonian city on the Via Egnatia and an Aegean port. Luke’s “politarchs” title has strong inscriptional support in Macedonia, making the civic setting historically distinctive.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for city, route, and church importance. Medium for exact house of Jason location.

**4. Major competing views**  
The duration of Paul’s stay may have been longer than the “three Sabbaths” synagogue sequence implies, because the letters suggest labor, teaching, and relationship depth.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Make Thessalonica a hostile-but-important urban hub: night escape, house raid, imperial loyalty accusations, letter follow-up about persecution and Christ’s return.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Port warehouses, Via Egnatia traffic, synagogue, Jason’s house, agora, magistrates, imperial cult symbolism, mixed Jewish-Gentile congregation.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 17; 1–2 Thessalonians; Via Egnatia sources; Macedonian inscriptions; F. F. Bruce, Ben Witherington.

## 5.15 Berea — Βέροια

**1. Biblical data**  
Berea’s Jews are described as more noble-minded, examining the Scriptures daily; many believe, including Greek women and men of high standing; Thessalonian opponents come and stir up crowds. Key text: Acts 17:10–15.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Berea was inland from the main coastal route, southwest of Thessalonica near foothill country.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for general location. Medium for exact route and synagogue site.

**4. Major competing views**  
No major location dispute, but Berea’s role is often underdeveloped. It may be better as a smaller study/refuge hub than a major city hub.

## 5.16 Athens — Ἀθῆναι

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul is provoked by the city’s idols, reasons in synagogue and agora, speaks at the Areopagus, engages Epicurean and Stoic philosophers, proclaims the unknown God, resurrection, repentance, and judgment. Key text: Acts 17:16–34.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Athens was no longer the political power it had been in the classical period, but it remained a symbolic intellectual and religious center full of temples, monuments, altars, schools, and civic memory.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Athens and Areopagus setting. Medium for the exact formal/legal nature of the Areopagus speech.

**4. Major competing views**  
Was Paul on the Areopagus hill, before the council, or in a broader intellectual forum? The text can be read in more than one way.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Athens should be a philosophical encounter hub, not a large church-growth hub. Focus on dialogue, observation, idols, public rhetoric, and mixed reception.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Agora, Acropolis view, altars, Stoic porch, philosophical schools, craftsmen, foreign visitors, low immediate danger but high ideological pressure.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 17; Pausanias; classical Athens archaeology; Bruce Winter; Keener.

## 5.17 Corinth and Cenchreae — Κόρινθος / Κεγχρεαί

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul stays in Corinth 18 months, works with Priscilla and Aquila, preaches in synagogue, moves next door to Titius Justus’s house, sees Crispus believe, appears before Gallio at the bema, and later writes major letters to the church. Cenchreae is connected to Phoebe and Paul’s vow. Key texts: Acts 18; Romans 16:1–2; 1–2 Corinthians.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Corinth controlled the isthmus between mainland Greece and the Peloponnese, with access to western and eastern harbors. The American School of Classical Studies at Athens identifies Ancient Corinth as its longest-running excavation, with Greek, Roman, and Byzantine remains in the city center where Paul preached. Source used in original response: American School of Classical Studies at Athens, Ancient Corinth — <https://www.ascsa.edu.gr/excavations/ancient-corinth>

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Corinth, Cenchreae, Gallio, and broad bema setting. Medium for exact house-church locations. High for Corinth’s importance to Pauline chronology because of Gallio.

**4. Major competing views**  
The Erastus inscription’s relation to Romans 16:23 is debated. Do not present it as certain. The social makeup of the Corinthian church is also debated: mixed status, not simply poor or elite.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Corinth is one of the best playable hubs: trade, ports, legal drama, house-church conflict, patronage, sexual immorality warnings, meat-market/idol temple dilemmas, spiritual gifts disorder, collection logistics.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Forum, bema, synagogue/house next door, workshops for tentmaking/leatherworking, Lechaion harbor road, Cenchreae port, temples, dining rooms, markets, sailors, merchants, freedmen, athletes/Isthmian games culture.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 18; Romans 16; 1–2 Corinthians; Gallio inscription; ASCSA Corinth; Jerome Murphy-O’Connor, *St. Paul’s Corinth*.

## 5.18 Ephesus — Ἔφεσος

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul briefly visits Ephesus, later returns and ministers there for an extended period. He teaches in the synagogue, then the school of Tyrannus; extraordinary miracles occur; magic books are burned; Demetrius the silversmith sparks a riot over Artemis; Paul later summons Ephesian elders to Miletus. Key texts: Acts 18:19–21; 19; 20:17–38; 1 Corinthians 15:32; 16:8–9; Ephesians; 1–2 Timothy.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Ephesus was capital of Roman Asia and a major harbor city, though its harbor landscape changed over time because of silting. UNESCO describes Ephesus as a major Hellenistic, Roman Imperial, and early Christian site, with the Artemis sanctuary among the largest and most powerful sanctuaries of the ancient world, and with Roman-period monuments such as the Celsus Library, terrace houses, harbor basin, and processional ways. Source used in original response: UNESCO, Ephesus — <https://whc.unesco.org/en/list/1018/>

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Ephesus as a major Pauline hub. High for Artemis cult/civic importance. Medium for exact school of Tyrannus location. Disputed for whether Ephesians was a circular letter rather than only to Ephesus.

**4. Major competing views**  
Ephesians may have been a circular letter to churches in Asia. The location of Paul’s imprisonment, if any Ephesian imprisonment is assumed, is debated.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Ephesus should be the largest non-Jerusalem playable hub: long-term ministry base, lecture hall, magic economy, idol-craft guilds, theater riot, regional church network.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Harbor road, theater, Artemis temple economy, agora, lecture hall, workshops, scroll/magic-text vendors, wealthy terrace houses, Asian provincial officials, routes to Colossae/Laodicea.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 18–20; 1 Corinthians 15–16; Ephesians; 1–2 Timothy; Revelation 2; UNESCO Ephesus; Austrian excavations; Clinton Arnold, Craig Keener.

## 5.19 Miletus — Μίλητος

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul avoids stopping at Ephesus on his return to Jerusalem but summons the Ephesian elders to Miletus. He gives a farewell speech warning of wolves and commending them to God. Key text: Acts 20:15–38.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Miletus was an important Ionian coastal city south of Ephesus, though its harbor conditions also changed through silting.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Miletus as the farewell location. Medium for exact meeting spot.

**4. Major competing views**  
No major location dispute; the bigger interpretive issue is why Paul bypassed Ephesus: time pressure for Pentecost, danger, or desire to avoid delay.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Make Miletus a powerful elder-council scene: dockside farewell, tears, warnings, church leadership mechanics.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Harbor, ships waiting, coastal roads, messengers sent to Ephesus, elders traveling down, open-air farewell.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 20; Barrington Atlas; Miletus archaeology; Keener.

## 5.20 Caesarea Maritima — Καισάρεια

**1. Biblical data**  
Caesarea is where Philip lives, where Agabus warns Paul, where Paul is held for two years, and where he appears before Felix, Festus, and Agrippa before appealing to Caesar. Key texts: Acts 8:40; 10; 21:8–16; 23–26.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Herod the Great built Caesarea’s major artificial harbor, Sebastos, between roughly 20 and 10 BCE. In 6 CE, Judea became a Roman province and the governmental/military residence moved from Jerusalem to Caesarea. Roman Ports summarizes the city’s harbor engineering, Herodian public buildings, Roman administrative role, and Josephus’s description of the harbor. Source used in original response: Roman Ports, “A Jewish Port for the Emperor” — <https://www.romanports.org/en/articles/ports-in-focus/542-a-jewish-port-for-the-emperor.html>

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Caesarea as Roman administrative hub and Paul’s imprisonment. Medium for exact prison quarters; “Herod’s praetorium” gives a general official setting.

**4. Major competing views**  
Exact locations of Paul’s hearings within the palace complex are reconstructed. Avoid claiming a specific room unless marked as interpretive.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Caesarea is the Roman legal hub: hearings, letters, prisoner transfer, governor politics, coastal military logistics, appeal-to-Caesar decision.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Artificial harbor, palace promontory, theater, hippodrome, aqueduct, barracks, archives, prison rooms, mixed Jewish-Gentile tensions, ships to Syria/Asia/Egypt/Rome.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 10, 21, 23–26; Josephus; Caesarea excavations; Roman Ports; Holum, Raban, Hohlfelder.

## 5.21 Malta — Μελίτη

**1. Biblical data**  
Paul’s shipwreck survivors reach Malta; islanders show kindness; Paul survives a viper bite; Publius hosts them; healings occur; after three months they sail onward. Key text: Acts 27:39–28:11.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
The traditional identification is Malta in the central Mediterranean. The ship then sails to Syracuse, Rhegium, and Puteoli, matching the westward route toward Italy in Acts 28.

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Malta as the traditional and dominant identification. Disputed at the level of exact bay/site of shipwreck.

**4. Major competing views**  
Competing shipwreck locations include St. Paul’s Bay, St. Thomas Bay/Munxar Reef proposals, and minority alternatives such as Melite/Meleda. Treat exact wreck site as disputed.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Malta should be a survival-and-hospitality chapter, not a normal mission city: storm aftermath, wet cold, islanders, healing, wintering, repairing travel network.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Rocky coast, reefs, winter weather, rural estates, local elites, fire scene, snake/viper event, stranded cargo, Alexandrian ship wintering.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 27–28; nautical studies of Acts 27; James Smith, *The Voyage and Shipwreck of St. Paul*; modern Malta archaeology and shipwreck debates.

## 5.22 Puteoli, Appian Way, Forum of Appius, Three Taverns, Rome

**1. Biblical data**  
After Malta, Paul stops at Syracuse and Rhegium, lands at Puteoli, stays seven days with believers, then travels toward Rome. Roman believers meet him at the Forum of Appius and Three Taverns. In Rome he lives under guard in rented quarters for two years, preaching the kingdom and teaching about Jesus. Key text: Acts 28:11–31; Romans.

**2. Historical/geographical data**  
Puteoli was a major Bay of Naples port. From there, Paul’s party would move north through Italian road systems toward Rome. Three Taverns was a stopping place on the Via Appia roughly thirty-three miles from Rome, and Forum Appii was another Appian Way station. Source used in original response: Biblical Training, “Three Taverns” — <https://www.biblicaltraining.org/library/three-taverns>

**3. Confidence level**  
High for Puteoli-to-Rome route generally. High for Acts’ named stopping places. Medium/low for exact Roman lodging location.

**4. Major competing views**  
Acts ends with house arrest, not martyrdom. Later traditions place Paul’s execution under Nero and sometimes associate Peter/Paul with specific Roman prison traditions, but those should be marked as later tradition.

**5. Game-design translation**  
Rome should feel like the imperial endgame but not a conquest hub: house arrest, visitors, letters, imperial rumor, Jewish leaders, Praetorian guard, gospel reaching Caesar’s household.

**6. Map/worldbuilding notes**  
Puteoli harbor, warehouses, grain ships, Appian paving, marshy sections, inns, Roman milestones, city gates, insulae, rented room, guards, Jewish community, imperial cult.

**7. Sources to verify**  
Acts 28; Romans; Philippians 1 and 4; 2 Timothy if accepted as final imprisonment; Tacitus and Suetonius for Nero-era Rome; Appian Way archaeology.

# 7. Revelation and Future Prophecy Handling

Revelation should not be treated like normal historical travel geography. Its seven churches are real Asia Minor churches, but the book is apocalyptic prophecy and vision. Use it as **future-facing expectation**, not as a normal Acts-era mission board.

## Correct handling

**Historical geography layer:**  
Ephesus, Smyrna, Pergamum, Thyatira, Sardis, Philadelphia, and Laodicea are real cities in Roman Asia.

**Prophetic/visionary layer:**  
John’s visions on Patmos, heavenly throne scenes, beasts, seals, bowls, Babylon imagery, New Jerusalem, and final judgment should be presented as revelation, not as ordinary explorable terrain.

**Gameplay implementation:**  
Unlock Revelation content through codex entries, visions, letters, prophecy scrolls, or later timeline chapters. Do not have the player “walk to the New Jerusalem” as if it is a first-century city.

**Pauline overlap:**  
Ephesus and Laodicea connect to Paul’s Asia network, but Revelation’s seven-church sequence is Johannine, not Pauline.
